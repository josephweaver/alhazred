# Alhazred Setting

## Alhazred

The canonical Western understanding of Alhazred begins with H. P. Lovecraft's Abdul Alhazred: the "Mad Arab," author of the *Necronomicon*, a half-legendary occultist reduced to an exotic caricature of frenzy, blasphemy, and forbidden knowledge. In that tradition, Alhazred is treated as a single doomed man whose work opened the door to cosmic horror.

That account is wrong in nearly every important respect.

In this setting, **Alhazred** is not primarily a personal name. It is a broken Western rendering of **al-hazr**, a phrase meaning, roughly, **the prohibition**, **the ban**, or **the forbidden restriction**. It named a protective tradition carried by scholars, physicians, astronomers, jurists, engineers, poets, translators, artisans, and mystics across the medieval Islamicate world. Its contributors wrote and argued in Arabic, Persian, Syriac, Hebrew, Greek, Latin, and other scholarly languages, crossing courts, libraries, observatories, hospitals, workshops, monasteries, and houses of worship.

Alhazred was not a single hidden order with one doctrine. It was a loose but disciplined network of observers and wardens who recognized that the world was periodically exposed to intelligences, geometries, diseases, and hungers from outside ordinary creation. They disagreed over theology, method, secrecy, and risk, but shared one practical purpose: to define what must not be opened.

For several generations, they were largely successful. They mapped hostile signs in the heavens, encoded countermeasures in architecture and verse, hid protective mathematics inside commentaries, and moved prohibitions along scholarly, religious, and trade networks. They did not defeat the cosmic powers permanently, but they delayed them, confused them, and gave human communities tools for survival.

The text known in the West as the *Necronomicon* is a damaged descendant of that work: a corrupted chain of excerpts, mistranslations, deliberate ciphers, colonial misunderstandings, and malicious interpolations. Protective instructions became invocations. Prohibitions became invitations. Ritual quarantines became summoning rites. The Western occult tradition inherited the ruin of a defense system and mistook it for a manual of power.

## The Illusion of Safety

The modern world is not safe. It has been made to feel safe.

The Great Old Ones and their attendant powers do not need humanity to understand the war being fought around it. Panic produces resistance, resistance produces organization, and organization gives the Alhazred prohibitions something to protect. It is more efficient to let humanity believe in continuity: governments, markets, families, schools, hospitals, weather reports, building codes, scheduled trains, working lights, and the expectation that tomorrow will resemble today.

That stability is a managed surface. Disasters are localized, records are corrected, witnesses are discredited, and impossible events are folded into fire, flood, riot, disease, accident, fraud, hallucination, or war. Humanity cannot win a war it does not know is being fought.

Alhazred artifacts puncture the false peace. They reveal that history, memory, and public reality have been edited to hide the scale of the conflict. The old prohibitions are not weapons of victory. They are acts of refusal: ways to delay, seal, misdirect, quarantine, preserve testimony, and keep small groups alive long enough to act while the rest of the world sleeps.

## Adventure Frame

Alhazred adventures begin with a present-day gathering.

Ordinary people come together around practical work: restoration, inspection, inheritance, cleanup, excavation, audit, renovation, cataloging, an estate sale, an insurance claim, a legal dispute, or an emergency repair. They are not yet occult investigators. They have jobs to do, obligations to meet, and mundane reasons to be in the same place.

During the work, someone finds an artifact hidden where it should not be: a document inside a wall, a ledger under floorboards, film in a foundation cavity, photographs behind an altar, a deposition in an archive, or a field report misfiled for decades. It is old enough to be historical, recent enough to be legible, and ordinary in every physical respect except one: its contents are impossible.

The artifact recounts an event erased from public history: an apocalypse, massacre, disappearance, civic collapse, impossible trial, failed expedition, quarantine, occult panic, or cosmic intrusion. It names places, institutions, rituals, victims, perpetrators, and survivors. By coincidence, impossibly, those survivors have the exact same names as the people who found the artifact.

Play then enters the recovered account. The adventure is the retelling of what those people did to survive, whether as prophecy, alternate history, contaminated record, ritual reenactment, inherited memory, or something worse.

## Historical Documents

Alhazred documents are not read like ordinary archives.

They can only be read in order, from first page to last. Pages beyond the current point cannot be skipped to for answers. They may appear blank, stuck together, water-damaged, redacted, overwritten, missing, or simply impossible to comprehend. The document permits sequence, not certainty.

That illegibility is not always damage. Often the page is unreadable because it contains every future outcome at once. Contradictory sentences, impossible marginalia, stains that almost form words, and ink that refuses to settle all mark events whose consequences have not yet collapsed into one history.

Unrealized future text often reads as fragmented non sequitur:

> The tree flies. Eye into the sun. Gunshot beneath the supper bell. A banquet in the subterranean valley. Teeth in the hymnbook. The witness drowns in the dry room. Three names crossed out, one name written twice.

As the characters act inside the recovered event, their actions become part of the record. Ink dries on old paper. Missing lines return. Margins fill with testimony. Damage resolves into legible text. The artifact is both historical object and active witness: old before it is opened, incomplete until it is lived.

This paradox is central to Alhazred play. The document does not remove agency by dictating the ending. It preserves whatever the characters actually do, then presents that preservation as something that was always waiting to be found.

## Character Death

> That is not dead which can eternal lie,  
> And with strange aeons even death may die.  
> -- H. P. Lovecraft

In Alhazred, character death is not a player-removal rule. It is a story engine.

When a character dies inside a recovered event, the death happens. Witnesses saw it. The wound was real. The body fell, drowned, burned, vanished, or was torn open. Death is never negated; it is answered.

At the end of the scene, the dead character is displaced back to the present-day frame. The player then works with the Guide to create a return fiction that lets the character re-enter the recovered event at the next plausible opening. The explanation must add to the world rather than erase what happened: a wolf spirit makes contact, a hag in the woods offers a bargain, a reanimator performs an obscene repair, a Great Old Thing refuses the death, a crossroads power takes a claim, or the document itself forces reality to compromise.

The character returns quickly, but with baggage. They may gain a scar, phobia, debt, patron, enemy, missing memory, altered body, spiritual claim, or other consequence that makes them stranger and more specific. These costs should rarely make the character less fun to play. They are boons disguised as banes: marks of survival that add depth, hooks, and conditional power while narrowing the character in small, meaningful ways.

Over time, repeated returns can make a character metaphysically overclaimed. Too many powers have bargains, marks, samples, names, or debts attached to them. Their death may become dangerous not because it ends the character, but because collecting on them would cause calamity among the things that believe they own a piece.

### Death as Design Stance

Many role-playing games treat the realistic threat of death as the proof that danger matters. Alhazred breaks from that assumption. The story is the objective. Death is real, witnessed, and consequential, but it is not primarily a removal mechanic.

When death becomes final at the table, it often produces one of two unsatisfying outcomes: the story ends, or the game invents a thin resurrection to keep going. Alhazred chooses a third path. Character death is a transformational critical moment. The character returns, but the fiction changes to explain why. Something intervenes, bargains, rewrites, claims, restores, or refuses the death.

The result is consequence without exclusion. The player stays in the game. The character carries new scars, debts, patrons, enemies, phobias, contradictions, and obligations. Death does not end the story; it gives the story another claim.

## Campaign Structure

Each artifact opens another glimpse past the veil.

The conventional Alhazred scenario is set in 1920s or 1930s Massachusetts, partly because that period preserves the Lovecraftian surface the setting is answering. This is a default, not a requirement. The same structure can carry the characters into any place or era where a record can survive: ancient Egyptian hieroglyphs, a medieval monastery ledger, an early modern court deposition, a colonial shipping manifest, a trench diary, or even a Paleolithic sequence preserved through translated cave drawings.

The present-day frame is the fixed track. Characters return to the apparent safety of the same world: the same continuity, the same managed normalcy, the same chain of impossible discoveries. That rigidity is part of the horror. The present is stable because it is the illusion.

The recovered past is where the campaign flexes. Each document opens a denied catastrophe whose details are not fully settled until the characters live through them. History is not fixed fact. It is a containment medium, edited by cosmic forces and rewritten through survival.

The first document is rarely complete. It leads to another record, another site, another contradiction, and another denied event. In game terms, each discovery becomes the next scenario. The same characters, or people bearing the same names, appear again in another time and place, faced with a new horror they must survive.

As the campaign advances, the repeated names should feel less like coincidence and more like structure. The characters are not merely reading the past. They are being aligned with it, inserted into it, or revealed as part of a recurring defensive pattern older than their present lives.

Every recovered account asks two questions: what did they do to survive, and what did survival cost?

The campaign culminates in the truth behind the present. Reality may feel safe because these people, across impossible histories, succeeded in pushing back the darkness. Or reality may only appear safe because they failed, and the present is the managed aftermath of defeat.

## Primary Setting: 1930 Massachusetts

The default recovered era for Alhazred is 1930 Massachusetts.

The First World War is still fresh in families, hospitals, factories, universities, and veterans' halls. The Great Depression is visible in breadlines, foreclosures, shuttered workshops, failing banks, transient camps, labor unrest, and quiet rooms where respectable people calculate how long they can keep up appearances. Beyond the city, agricultural collapse and dust storms turn farms into debt, hunger, migration, and rumors of fields where nothing wholesome will grow.

The human world is already dangerous. Race, ethnicity, religion, class, gender, immigration status, and labor politics shape who is believed, protected, policed, hired, housed, treated, blamed, or allowed to disappear. Racial segregation, antisemitism, nativism, eugenics, red scares, strikebreaking, and everyday institutional cruelty are not side details; they are part of the pressure that lets worse things hide. The cosmic horror does not replace human cruelty. It exploits it, and competes with it for the privilege of being the worst thing in the room.

Then, just when things could not get worse, came the things that should not be.

This is not a candlelit antiquarian world. Characters should expect 1930s technology: revolvers, shotguns, rifles, early automatics, automobiles, trucks, streetcars, trains, telegraphs, switchboards, electric lights, cameras, phonographs, industrial tools, printing presses, and useful but limited medicine. Violence is faster than in the deep past, but help is still slow, records are fragile, and distance still matters.

Boston is the primary anchor rather than Arkham: dense neighborhoods, old cemeteries, harbors, churches, synagogues, libraries, hospitals, newspapers, machine politics, immigrant communities, old money, desperate labor, and institutions old enough to hide things inside their walls. The city should feel grounded at street level and impossible at the edges.

The surrounding world is deliberately compressed. A short rail trip or coastal road can reach fishing towns, decaying mill cities, private schools, sanitariums, cranberry bogs, pine barrens, marshes, observatories, abandoned estates, military ruins, and villages older than the map admits. Lovecraftian-like locales may exist, but as echoes, rivals, or replacements rather than direct dependence on canonical Arkham geography.

The campaign can use fictional universities and elite schools instead of Miskatonic University or other inherited names. These institutions preserve the academic horror surface while creating their own archives, scandals, occult collections, rivalries, and compromised benefactors.

The compressed 1930 setting is a night of convergence. Horrors that should belong to separate traditions and nightmares press into one historical moment: Poe-like guilt, Lovecraftian cosmic intrusion, survivals from deep prehistory, industrial nightmares, and theorems that should not describe living things. The result is not spectacle, but pressure: too many impossible systems trying to become true at once.

### Example: The Temple Manuscript

A small group oversees the remodeling of a very old holy temple that has fallen into disrepair. The building has passed through many hands and many names. Parts of it are older than the records say they should be. The work begins with permits, budgets, structural surveys, dust, rot, wiring, stone, plaster, and the ordinary exhaustion of repairing a sacred place history has nearly abandoned.

During the gutting of an interior wall, one construction worker finds a brittle manuscript, ledger, or bound report hidden in a cavity. Its paper, ink, and binding suggest the early twentieth century. It recounts an apocalypse that could not have happened in 1920s or 1930s Massachusetts: cosmic intrusions, impossible geometries, public madness, panic, civic collapse, and the near-total extermination of the human race.

It also describes a small group of survivors.

They have the exact same names as the construction workers.
