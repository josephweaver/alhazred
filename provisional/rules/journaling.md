# Journaling and Player Notes

## Overview

Many players naturally:
- take notes,
- journal events,
- sketch locations,
- draw maps,
- track clues,
- or record conversations during play.

The 24-System may optionally elevate these real-world player artifacts into in-game objects.

Participation is always voluntary.

---

# Opt-In Journaling

The Guide may ask whether a player wishes to treat their real journal, notebook, or map as an in-world object.

The player may:
- opt-in,
- or opt-out.

Both approaches are fully valid.

---

# Opt-In Advantages

When a player opts in:

- their notes may function as character records,
- maps may exist physically in-world,
- written observations may act as evidence,
- and journals may become interactive props.

This can:
- deepen immersion,
- reinforce memory,
- support investigation,
- and create meaningful physical artifacts during play.

The Guide may occasionally reference:
- missing notes,
- unclear sketches,
- damaged journals,
- or incomplete observations
as narrative elements.

---

# Opt-In Risks

An in-world journal may:
- be lost,
- stolen,
- damaged,
- discovered by enemies,
- or contain dangerous information.

Players should understand that treating notes as in-world objects introduces narrative vulnerability.

The Guide should apply this carefully and primarily when dramatically meaningful.

---

# Opt-Out Journaling

If a player opts out:

- notes remain player aids only,
- journals are not considered in-world objects,
- and the character is assumed to remember relevant information reasonably.

This approach supports:
- accessibility,
- reduced stress,
- lighter bookkeeping,
- and different player styles.

Players should never feel punished for opting out.

---

# Maps and Sketches

Players may optionally:
- sketch environments,
- track routes,
- map dangerous areas,
- or diagram relationships.

If opted in:
- these maps may become physical game objects,
- contain inaccuracies,
- or influence navigation and investigation.

The Guide should reward:
- clever observation,
- spatial reasoning,
- and player engagement.

---

# Horror and Journaling

In horror-oriented campaigns, journals create powerful opportunities for:
- fragmented observations,
- contradictory records,
- unstable memory,
- incomplete diagrams,
- and physical evidence of psychological decline.

However, the system should avoid weaponizing journaling in ways that punish player participation.

The primary goal is:
- immersion,
- engagement,
- and collaborative storytelling.

---

# Design Philosophy

The purpose of journaling rules is to transform existing player behavior into meaningful optional gameplay.

The system should reward engagement without requiring it.

A player who keeps detailed notes may gain:
- stronger immersion,
- better investigative continuity,
- and memorable physical props.

A player who prefers not to journal should remain equally capable of meaningful play.
