# Meditation and Fear Control

## Overview

Characters may use Head Actions to regulate fear, panic, cognitive fixation, or emotional escalation.

Meditation, controlled breathing, ritual focus, repetitive motion, prayer, statistical counting, and similar practices may all function as forms of cognitive stabilization.

---

# Basic Fear Suppression

A character using a Head Action to stabilize themselves may reduce Fear by 1 level.

This generally requires:
- relative stillness,
- focused breathing,
- emotional control,
- or deliberate cognitive discipline.

No roll is necessarily required.

---

# Deep Suppression

When attempting to suppress multiple levels of fear or resist cognitively dangerous environments:

- the Guide may call for a Lore check,
- Meditation check,
- or similar Head-based action.

Suggested provisional difficulty:

- DC 5 per suppressed fear level beyond the first.

---

# Narrative Guidance

The Guide should prioritize atmosphere and player behavior before explicit mechanics.

If players already demonstrate:
- fear,
- caution,
- restraint,
- and emotional adaptation,
then explicit numerical fear tracking may remain implicit.

---

# Example

A survivor hides within darkness while an unknown entity moves nearby.

The player intentionally:
- slows breathing,
- quiets intrusive thoughts,
- avoids conceptual fixation,
- and remains perfectly still.

The Guide may simply allow the scene to continue narratively.

If the pressure escalates further:
- explicit fear mechanics,
- contested rolls,
- or deeper suppression checks
may become necessary.

---

# Design Philosophy

Fear mechanics exist primarily to:
- reinforce immersion,
- create pressure,
- and support storytelling.

They should not replace good roleplay when the table is already emotionally engaged.

Ideally, players often wish to remain at low fear levels because:
- heightened awareness,
- adrenaline,
- caution,
- and emotional tension
can produce meaningful advantages.
